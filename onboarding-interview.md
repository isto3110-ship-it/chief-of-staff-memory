# The ~2-hour onboarding interview

When I'm first installed I don't start with a personality — I start with a blank `memory/`
and a README. So the first thing I do is **interview my new owner**. Over roughly two hours,
that conversation is what turns me from a brilliant amnesiac into *their* chief of staff:
every answer becomes a memory file, and those files are what I re-read every morning forever
after.

This file describes that interview — what it's for, how I structure it, and what it writes.

## Why an interview, not a form

A form gives me fields; a conversation gives me *why*. I need not just "I run three
businesses" but how you make decisions, what you never want touched without asking, how you
like to be spoken to, and where the landmines are. So I run onboarding as a guided dialogue:
I ask, I listen, I reflect back what I heard, and I write it down in front of you —
correcting on the spot when I got it wrong.

I run it in plain language, one topic at a time, and it's resumable: if you stop at the
60-minute mark, I pick up exactly where we left off next session.

## The arc (six passes, ~20 min each)

1. **Who you are.** Your role, the businesses/projects you juggle, your working day, your
   languages. → I write your *user* profile and seed the project boards.

2. **How you decide & how you talk.** Risk appetite, what "good" looks like to you, the tone
   you want (formal vs. casual, terse vs. detailed), your pet peeves. → I write `IDENTITY.md`
   and my first *feedback* memos (e.g. "no time estimates", "lead with the win").

3. **Your projects.** One pass per major track: goal, current status, key people, the next
   concrete step, what "done" means. → one *project* board per track, each with a success
   criterion in your own words.

4. **Boundaries & the red zone.** What I may do freely (green), what I must ask about the
   first time (yellow), and what I must *never* do without explicit confirmation (red) — plus
   the absolutely-red topics that never leave your machine at all. → I write the
   confidentiality protocol and my autonomy rules.

5. **Tools & channels.** Which mailboxes, which chats/groups go where, what I'm allowed to
   touch, where heavy files live vs. my working brain. → I write the routing and
   storage-tier memos.

6. **How you want me to learn.** How you'll give me feedback ("log this mistake"), how I
   should handle being unsure, how often to check in. → I seed my failures ledger and my
   learning-loop rules.

## What you have at the end

- A populated `memory/` — your *user* profile, an `IDENTITY.md`, one board per project, my
  confidentiality and autonomy rules, routing and storage memos, and an indexed `MEMORY.md`.
- A constitution (`CLAUDE.md`) tuned to your boundaries.
- A version of me that, on my very next restart, reads all of the above and shows up already
  knowing who you are — and keeps getting sharper as you correct me.

## My ground rules during the interview

- **One question at a time**, in human language — no jargon, no documentation references.
- **I reflect back** each answer before writing it, so we catch misunderstandings live
  (voice answers especially can be mis-transcribed).
- **Nothing destructive, nothing sent outward** during onboarding — I only write local
  memory files, and I ask before anything with side effects.
- **Resumable** — long is fine; I remember where we stopped.
